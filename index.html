The challenge

What is RCJA Soccer?

In RCJA Soccer, teams of two autonomous robots compete on a green carpet field to score goals using an infrared ball. Robots must be built from LEGO, programmed by the students, and started manually — no remote control allowed. It is one of the most popular RoboCup Junior challenges in Australia.

RoboCup Junior Australia

This guide focuses on the two LEGO-based leagues: Simple Simon (beginner) and Standard (step up). Both use the same IR ball, the same field, and the same game rules. The differences are in what sensors and wheels your robot is allowed to use.

Simple Simon Beginner
  • LEGO construction only
  • IR sensor only (from approved list)
  • No gyro or compass sensors
  • No omni-directional wheels
  • No dribbler allowed
  • 2-year participation limit
  • 1.0 kg max weight
Standard League Step up
  • LEGO construction only
  • Approved 3rd-party sensors allowed
  • Gyro and compass sensors allowed
  • Omni wheels allowed (80 mm max, LEGO axle)
  • No dribbler allowed
  • No participation time limit
  • 1.0 kg max weight
Both leagues use the same IR ball, field, and game rules. The main differences are sensors and wheels. Simple Simon is a great starting point — once you have up to two years of experience, Standard League opens up more options for your robot design.

Playing environment

The Soccer Field

The field is a green or charcoal carpet with matte black walls, two coloured goals, and white boundary lines. Robots play on the carpet inside the white lines — the area between the lines and the walls is the "out area". Fields may be on a table or on the floor.

Green carpet
3–5 mm thick green or charcoal coloured carpet. The recommended surface for all RCJA Soccer fields. All dimensions have a 5% tolerance.
Surface
Black walls
Matte black walls surround the entire field, including behind the goals. Walls are at least 100 mm high (220 mm at Nationals).
Boundary
Goals
450 mm wide, 74 mm deep, with a crossbar 140 mm high. One goal is cyan (Dulux Striking Cyan), the other is yellow (Dulux Dandelion Yellow).
Scoring
White lines
50 mm thick white lines, 250 mm from the walls on every side. These define the boundary of the out area.
Boundary
Neutral points
Two side neutral points plus one centre point. The ball is placed on the nearest neutral point after stoppages like lack of progress.
Restart
Out area
The space between the white lines and the walls. Ball-out-of-play rules differ between leagues (see below).

Ball out of play

The rules for when the ball is "out" differ between Simple Simon / Standard and the higher leagues:

League Ball is out of play when...
Simple Simon The ball strikes the wall behind either goal. If inclines are not used, the ball strikes any wall
Standard If inclines are not used: the ball strikes any wall (including sides of goals). If inclines are used: the ball strikes the back wall or sides of goals
What are inclines? The out areas of the field may be raised by 10 mm on the outside edge, creating a gentle slope that rolls the ball back toward the playing area. Whether inclines are used affects ball-out-of-play rules.

How the game works

Game Rules at a Glance

These are the key rules for Simple Simon and Standard League. Both leagues follow the same game rules — the differences are only in robot construction. For the complete rules, use the RCJA Rules Reference Tool.

Key rule concepts

Ball capture zone
30 mm maximum depth
Any concavity in your robot (measured with a straight edge across protruding points) cannot be deeper than 30 mm. Referees test this during scrutineering and can check at any time.
No holding the ball
All degrees of freedom
A robot cannot take full control of the ball — no surrounding it, encircling it, or trapping it. If the ball stops rolling while the robot moves, it's probably being held.
No dribbler
Simple Simon & Standard
A dribbler is a rotating drum that puts backspin on the ball. These are not allowed in Simple Simon or Standard League. Only Lightweight and Open leagues permit dribblers.
Goalie rules
Must move forward
A goalie must respond to the ball with forward movement down the field. It cannot just move sideways. Failure to move forward will result in the robot being deemed "damaged".
Multiple defence
One defender in the box
Only one robot from the defending team should be in the penalty area. If two defenders are in the box and affecting play, the less influential one is moved to the centre.
Fouls
Attacking without the ball
If a robot continuously attacks or charges a robot that doesn't have the ball, the referee calls a foul. The fouling robot is deemed damaged. Repeated fouls lead to disqualification.

Other important rules

  • 10-goal mercy rule: A game ends when there is a 10-goal difference. The losing team may elect to continue, but the score won't change.
  • Late penalty: Teams lose one goal per minute if late to a match. After 10 minutes, the game is forfeited 10–0.
  • No free kicks, penalty kicks, or offside — the game is simpler than real football.
  • Communication: Robots can communicate wirelessly, but it must not interfere with other robots. Use unique message prefixes. The referee can ask you to disable communication.
  • Humans: Only the team captain can handle robots during the game, and only at the referee's instruction. Other team members must stay more than 1 metre from the field.
The referee's decisions are final. Arguing with a referee results in a yellow warning card. Continued argument means a red card and immediate forfeit. If you disagree with a call, note it calmly — protests can only be about scoring errors, and only before signing the score sheet.
01
Autonomous only
No remote control of any kind. Robots run downloaded programs and must be started manually by the team captain. They cannot be started from a laptop, tablet, or phone.
02
2 x 5-minute halves
Five-minute break between halves. The game clock runs continuously except during a referee's timeout. Some competitions may use 10-minute halves.
03
Kick-off
Coin toss decides who kicks off or chooses ends. Ball placed at centre, non-kicking team's robots must have part of the robot in their penalty box. All robots start on the referee's command.
04
Scoring
A goal is scored when the ball strikes the back wall of the goal. Own goals count for the opposition. After a goal, the non-scoring team gets the kick-off.
05
Lack of progress
Called when the ball is stuck, no robot can reach it, or a robot is "forcing" the ball through a defender. Ball moves to the nearest neutral point (then centre if it happens again).
06
Damaged robots
A robot is "damaged" if it doesn't respond to the ball, gets stuck for 20+ seconds, tips over, damages the ball or field, or makes noise from a speaker. Off the field for at least 30 seconds.

Key components

SPIKE Hub
The brain of your robot. Runs your program, reads sensors, and drives motors. Must be started manually — no laptops or phones.

Motors
SPIKE large and medium motors for driving wheels and mechanisms. Your robot must be able to drive in all directions.

Required
Colour sensor
Can detect field colours (green carpet, white lines) and goal colours (cyan vs yellow). Useful for orientation and knowing which goal to aim for.
Optional
Gyro / compass
Standard League only. Helps maintain heading and orientation so your robot always knows which way it's facing. Not allowed in Simple Simon.
Standard only
Battery
Must use the LEGO rechargeable battery only. No other power sources or voltages are permitted in Simple Simon and Standard.
Required
Handle requirement: Every robot must have a stable, easily accessible handle at least 50 mm above the highest structure. Cable ties make a strong, lightweight handle. The handle height does not count toward the 220 mm robot height limit.

Approved sensors: Only sensors on the official list are allowed. Check the current list at robocupjunior.org.au/soccer/sensors.

The 220 mm cylinder test: Your robot must rotate freely inside a 220 mm diameter cylinder with no resistance. Build to 210 mm to allow for tolerance. Any moving parts must be fully extended during inspection.

Design guidance

Building Your Soccer Robot

Practical tips for designing and building a competitive SPIKE-based soccer robot. Whether you are building for Simple Simon or Standard League, these fundamentals apply.

Robot roles

Most teams run two robots with different jobs. You don't have to — both robots can play the same role — but specialising usually works better.

Striker
Ball-chasing robot
Finds the ball using the IR sensor, drives toward it, and pushes it toward the opponent's goal. Needs to be fast and responsive. A good striker reacts quickly to changes in ball position.
Goalie
Goal defender
Stays near your goal but MUST move forward to intercept the ball. Cannot just sit in front of the goal or only move sideways. A goalie that doesn't move forward will be deemed "damaged" by the referee.
Goalie warning: A goalie that only moves sideways or stays stationary will be removed from the field as a "damaged" robot. It must respond to the ball with forward movement — the rules require it to be able to take some part of the robot outside the penalty box (300 mm from the goal).

Things people forget

  • The handle — every robot needs one, at least 50 mm above the top. Add it early, not the night before.
  • Cable management — loose wires catch on walls and other robots. Tidy them with cable ties.
  • Battery access — you need to charge between matches. Don't bury the battery deep inside the robot.
  • Calibration access — you may need to adjust sensors at the venue. Make sure you can reach them without disassembling the robot.
  • Speakers — if your SPIKE hub makes sounds during gameplay (not just debugging), your robot will be deemed damaged. Turn off any startup sounds.
01
Start simple
Get a robot that drives and finds the ball before adding complexity. A working simple robot beats a broken complex one every time. You can always iterate between matches.
02
Ball capture zone
Keep any concavity under 30 mm deep. Referees test this by placing a straight edge across the outermost points of the robot. If the ball can go in more than 30 mm, you fail inspection.
03
Weight budget
1 kg maximum including everything. LEGO adds up quickly — weigh your robot early and often. Leave room for the battery and any sensors you still need to add.
04
Size matters
220 mm diameter cylinder, 220 mm height. Build to 210 mm to give yourself tolerance. Remember: moving parts must be fully extended during the cylinder test.
05
Wheels and drive
Simple Simon: standard LEGO wheels only. Standard: omni wheels allowed (80 mm max diameter, must use a LEGO axle). Your robot must be able to drive in all directions.
06
Protect your wires
Cable ties or tape can secure wires but must not be part of the robot's structure. No glue, screws, or non-LEGO building materials are permitted.

Programming concepts

Programming Your Robot

SPIKE robots can be programmed in SPIKE App blocks (drag-and-drop) or Python. This section covers the key programming concepts for soccer — the ideas your code needs to handle, regardless of which language you use.

SPIKE App Blocks Beginner
Visual drag-and-drop programming in the LEGO Education SPIKE app. Great for getting started — you can see the logic flow and test quickly.
  • No syntax to memorise
  • Built-in motor and sensor blocks
  • Quick to prototype and iterate
  • Works on tablets and computers
Python Step up
Text-based programming for more control and flexibility. SPIKE supports MicroPython. Better for complex logic and fine-tuned sensor handling.
  • More precise control over timing
  • Easier to handle complex conditions
  • Transferable programming skill
  • Better for inter-robot communication

Core programming concepts for soccer

Your robot's program needs to handle these key tasks. The exact implementation will depend on your hardware and language choice, but every soccer robot needs to solve these problems.

01
Sense the ball
Use the IR sensor to detect the ball's direction and relative distance. The ball pulses infrared light at 40 kHz — your sensor reads the strength from different angles to work out where the ball is.
02
Drive toward it
Convert sensor readings into motor commands. Turn toward the strongest IR signal and drive forward. The faster your loop runs, the more responsive your robot will be.
03
Know your heading
Standard League can use a gyro to maintain orientation — so the robot always knows which way is "forward". Simple Simon teams need to rely on other strategies like motor timing or colour detection.
04
Goal awareness
Know which goal is yours. A colour sensor pointing forward can distinguish cyan from yellow. Without this, your robot might score own goals — which count for the opposition.
05
Avoid penalties
Program a "stuck" detection: if motors are running but the robot isn't moving (e.g. stalled against a wall), reverse and turn. A robot stuck for 20+ seconds in the goal area gets deemed "damaged".
06
Communication
Your two robots can communicate wirelessly (e.g. Bluetooth). Use unique message prefixes so you don't pick up signals from other teams. The referee can ask you to disable this at any time.
Manual start only: Your robot must be started by pressing a button on the hub — not from a laptop, tablet, or phone. Make sure your start sequence works reliably from a physical button press, every time.

Annotated code: You must submit annotated code before the competition. Judges will ask you to explain your program — make sure every team member understands how it works, not just the person who wrote it.

At the competition

Competition Day Preparation

Knowing the rules and having a good robot is only half the battle. Being prepared for competition day — scrutineering, calibration, interviews, and match logistics — makes a big difference to how your team performs.

Scrutineering
First thing each morning
Robots are checked for size (220 mm cylinder), weight (1 kg max), ball capture zone (30 mm), and construction legality. Happens every morning. If you modify your robot during the day, you must get re-inspected.
Calibration
Before each game
At least 5 minutes of setup time before each match. Use this to calibrate IR sensors and colour sensors for the venue's lighting and magnetic conditions. Every venue is different.
Team interview
During the competition
A panel will interview your team about your design, programming, and process. You must demonstrate that the robot is your own work and that every team member understands the code.
Round robin
Group stage
3 points for a win, 1 for a draw, 0 for a loss. Teams ranked by total points, then goals scored, then goal difference. Play fair and learn from every match.
Finals
Top teams
Play-offs for 1st through 4th. Tied finals use "golden goal" — play continues until someone scores. After 5 extra minutes with no goal, only one robot per team is allowed on the field.

Preparation checklist

01
Annotated code
Submit before the competition. Your code must have comments explaining sensor use, decision-making logic, and motor control. You can keep updating after submission, but maintain an annotated version.
02
Digital poster
A3 size. Must include: team name, member first names and roles, one innovative feature, one challenge you overcame, and what you learned. Required for special awards. No personal info beyond first names.
03
Spare parts
Bring extra LEGO pieces, cables, and a charger. You can repair between matches, but modifications must be re-inspected. Don't get caught without a critical part.
04
Practice on carpet
Sensors behave differently on different surfaces. If possible, practice on carpet similar to competition carpet (3–5 mm thick, green or charcoal). The Gen2 vinyl mat works for practice.
05
Know your roles
Designate a captain — they handle robots during the game. Other members start one robot each, then must stay 1+ metre from the field. Cover any cyan, yellow, or orange on your clothing if asked.
06
Spirit of competition
Share what you've learned with other teams. Ask questions. Be respectful to referees — their decisions are final. The goal is learning, not just winning.
Late penalty: Teams lose one goal per minute if late to a match. After 10 minutes, the game is forfeited 10–0. The 10 minutes includes any break time — be ready early.

After the game: If both captains are happy with the result, they sign the score sheet. After signing, no protests can be lodged — so check the score before you sign.
